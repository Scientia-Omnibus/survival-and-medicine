> After having solved the problems of finding water, shelter, and animal food, you will have to consider the use of plants you can eat. In a survival situation you should always be on the lookout for familiar wild foods and live off the land whenever possible.
>
> You must not count on being able to go for days without food as some sources would suggest. Even in the most static survival situation, maintaining health through a complete and nutritious diet is essential to maintaining strength and peace of mind.
>
> Nature can provide you with food that will let you survive almost any ordeal, if you don't eat the wrong plant. Plants can provide you with medicines, weapons, raw materials for shelters, fire-building materials, chemicals for poisoning fish, preserving animal hides, and for camouflage.

### EDIBILITY OF PLANTS

Plants are valuable food sources — widely available, easily procured, and can meet all your nutritional needs.

> **WARNING: The critical factor in using plants for food is to avoid accidental poisoning. Eat only those plants you can positively identify and know are safe to eat.**

**Precautions when collecting wild plants:**

| Precaution | Reason/Action |
|---|---|
| Pesticide contamination | Plants near homes, buildings, roadsides may be sprayed. Wash thoroughly. Avoid roadside plants due to exhaust. |
| Contaminated water | Plants in water with Giardia or other parasites are contaminated. Boil or disinfect. |
| Fungal toxins | Do not eat fruit starting to spoil or showing mildew/fungus. |
| Genetic variation | Same species may differ in toxin levels (e.g., chokecherry). Avoid any weed, leaves, or seeds with almondlike scent (cyanide compounds). |
| Individual sensitivity | If prone to gastric distress, avoid unknown wild plants. If sensitive to poison ivy, avoid sumacs, mangoes, cashews. |
| Bitterness (tannins) | Boil in several changes of water to remove bitter compounds (acorns, water lily rhizomes). |
| Oxalates (oxalic acid) | Produce burning sensation in mouth/throat; damage kidneys. Baking, roasting, or drying destroys oxalate crystals. |

> **WARNING: Do not eat mushrooms in a survival situation! The only way to tell if a mushroom is edible is by positive identification. There is no room for experimentation. Symptoms from the most dangerous mushrooms may not show until days after ingestion.**

### PLANT IDENTIFICATION

Identify plants by leaf shape, leaf margin, leaf arrangement, and root structure.

**Leaf margins:**

| Margin Type | Description |
|---|---|
| Toothed | Serrated edge |
| Lobed | Deeply divided sections |
| Smooth/toothless | Continuous edge |

**Leaf shapes:**

Lance-shaped, elliptical, egg-shaped, oblong, wedge-shaped, triangular, long-pointed, or top-shaped.

**Leaf arrangements:**

| Arrangement | Description |
|---|---|
| Opposite | Leaves paired at each node |
| Alternate | Single leaf at each node, alternating sides |
| Compound | Multiple leaflets on one stem |
| Simple | Single leaf blade |
| Basal rosette | Leaves at base in circular pattern |

**Root structures:**

| Root Type | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Taproot | Single main root, usually one plant stalk per root | Carrots |
| Tuber | Fleshy underground stem found on strings or clusters | Potatoes, daylilies |
| Bulb | Concentric rings when sliced | Onions, wild onions |
| Clove | Bulblike, separates into small pieces | Garlic, wild garlic |
| Rhizome | Large creeping rootstock, many plants from "eyes" | |
| Corm | Solid when cut (no rings) | |
| Crown | Mophead structure under soil surface | Asparagus |

Some plants have both edible and poisonous parts. Many are edible only at certain times of the year. Some have poisonous relatives that look very similar.

### UNIVERSAL EDIBILITY TEST

If you have any doubt about a plant's edibility, apply this test before eating any portion.

| Phase | Step | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Preparation | 1 | Test only one part of a potential food plant at a time |
| Preparation | 2 | Separate plant into basic components: leaves, stems, roots, buds, flowers |
| Preparation | 3 | Smell for strong or acid odors (smell alone does not indicate edibility) |
| Preparation | 4 | Do not eat for 8 hours before starting the test |
| Contact test | 5 | Place a piece of the plant part on your inner elbow or wrist for 15 minutes to check for contact poisoning |
| Oral test | 6 | During the test, take nothing by mouth except purified water and the plant part |
| Oral test | 7 | Select a small portion of a single part and prepare it the way you plan to eat it |
| Oral test | 8 | Touch a small pinch to your outer lip to test for burning or itching |
| Oral test | 9 | If no reaction after 3 minutes, place the plant part on your tongue for 15 minutes |
| Oral test | 10 | If no reaction, thoroughly chew a pinch and hold in mouth for 15 minutes. Do not swallow. |
| Oral test | 11 | If no burning, itching, numbing, stinging, or other irritation, swallow the food |
| Waiting period | 12 | Wait 8 hours. If any ill effects, induce vomiting and drink lots of water |
| Confirmation | 13 | If no ill effects, eat 0.25 cup of same part prepared same way. Wait another 8 hours. If no ill effects, it is safe. |

> **CAUTION: Test all parts of the plant. A part that is edible cooked may not be edible raw. Different individuals may have different reactions.**

**Plants to avoid (potential toxicity indicators):**

| Characteristic | Examples/Notes |
|---|---|
| Milky or discolored sap | |
| Beans, bulbs, or seeds inside pods | |
| Bitter or soapy taste | |
| Spines, fine hairs, or thorns | |
| Foliage resembling dill, carrot, parsnip, or parsley | |
| Almond scent in woody parts and leaves | Cyanide compounds |
| Grain heads with pink, purplish, or black spurs | |
| Three-leafed growth pattern | |

These criteria help avoid potentially toxic plants but may also eliminate some edible ones.

### EDIBLE PLANTS BY ZONE

**Temperate Zone:**

Amaranth, arrowroot, asparagus, beechnut, blackberries, blueberries, burdock, cattail, chestnut, chicory, chufa, dandelion, daylily, nettle, oaks, persimmon, plantain, pokeweed, prickly pear cactus, purslane, sassafras, sheep sorrel, strawberries, thistle, water lily and lotus, wild onion and garlic, wild rose, wood sorrel.

**Tropical Zone:**

Bamboo, bananas, breadfruit, cashew nut, coconut, mango, palms, papaya, sugarcane, taro.

**Desert Zone:**

Acacia, agave, cactus (various), date palm, desert amaranth.

### SEAWEEDS

Valuable source of iodine, minerals, and vitamin C. Large quantities can have a severe laxative effect.

| Type | Preparation |
|---|---|
| Dulse, green seaweed, laver | Dry thin/tender varieties in sun or over fire until crisp. Crush and add to soups/broths. |
| Irish moss, kelp, sugar wrack | Boil thick leathery varieties briefly to soften. Eat as vegetable or with other foods. |
| Mojaban | Some varieties edible raw after testing |

Gather living plants attached to rocks or floating free. Discard seaweed washed ashore (may be spoiled). Dry freshly harvested seaweed for later use.

### PREPARATION OF PLANT FOOD

| Method | Plants | Technique |
|---|---|---|
| Boiling | Leaves, stems, buds | Boil until tender. Change water if necessary to remove bitterness. |
| Boiling/baking/roasting | Tubers and roots | Drying helps remove caustic oxalates from some roots (Arum family). |
| Leaching | Acorns, bitter nuts | Crush, place in strainer, pour boiling water through or immerse in running water. |
| Roasting | Chestnuts, some nuts | Enhances flavor. Can be eaten raw. |
| Grinding into meal/flour | Hard/dry grains and seeds | Boil or grind when matured. |
| Boiling down sap | Maple, birch, walnut, sycamore | Boil sap down to syrup. Takes ~35 liters of maple sap for 1 liter of syrup. |

### PLANTS FOR MEDICINE

> **WARNING: Positive identification is as critical for medicinal use as for food.**

**Terms and definitions:**

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Poultice | Crushed leaves or plant parts, possibly heated, applied to wound directly or wrapped in cloth. As it dries, draws toxins out. Apply as warm as patient can stand. |
| Infusion / tisane / tea | Small quantity of herb in container, pour hot water over, steep (covered or uncovered). Use caution on empty stomach. |
| Decoction | Boiled-down or simmered herb leaf or root. Typical ratio: 28-56g herb to 0.5L water. |
| Expressed juice | Liquids or sap squeezed from plant material, applied to wound or made into medicine. |

Natural remedies work slower than modern medicines. Start with smaller doses and allow more time.

**Specific remedies (survival use only — some are potentially toxic with long-term use):**

| Condition | Plant Remedy | How to Use |
|---|---|---|
| Diarrhea | Blackberry roots, white oak bark, clay, ashes, charcoal, powdered chalk, pectin | Drink tea from roots/bark. Mix powdered substances in tannic acid tea; 2 tbsp every 2 hours. Pectin from citrus rind or apple pomace. |
| Bleeding | Plantain leaves, yarrow (Achillea millefolium), prickly pear (raw peeled), witch hazel, sweet gum | Apply to wound for astringent effect. Sweet gum chewed or used as toothpick for bleeding gums. |
| Antiseptic | Wild onion/garlic (expressed juice), chickweed leaves, dock leaves, burdock root (decoction), mallow, white oak bark, prickly pear, slippery elm, yarrow, sugar, honey | External use only. Sugar: apply until syrupy, wash off, reapply. Honey: apply 3x daily. Best antiseptic for open wounds and burns. |
| Fever | Willow bark tea, elder flowers/fruit (infusion), linden flower tea, aspen/slippery elm bark (decoction), yarrow tea, peppermint tea | Drink as tea |
| Colds/sore throats | Plantain leaves or willow bark (decoction), burdock roots tea, mallow/mullein flowers or roots, yarrow or mint leaves | Drink as tea or decoction |
| Aches/pains/sprains | Dock, plantain, chickweed, willow bark, garlic, sorrel (poultice). Sweet gum. Willow bark tea or chewing. Salves of expressed juices in animal fat or oil. | External poultice or internal tea. Willow bark contains raw aspirin component. |
| Itching/contact dermatitis | Jewelweed (Impatiens biflora), witch hazel, aloe vera, dandelion sap, garlic, sweet gum, plantain leaves, tobacco | Apply poultice or juice. Jewelweed is best for poison ivy, rashes, insect stings, sunburn. Tobacco deadens nerve endings (toothaches). |
| Sedative | Mint leaves tea, passionflower leaves tea | Drink as tea for sleep |
| Hemorrhoids | Elm bark or oak bark tea (external wash), plantain leaf juice, Solomon's seal root decoction, tannic acid, witch hazel | External application |
| Heat rash | Tannic acid, witch hazel, cornstarch or crushed nonpoisonous plant powder | Apply to dry out rash after cleansing |
| Constipation | Dandelion leaves decoction, rose hips, walnut bark, raw daylily flowers | Drink decoctions or eat flowers. Large amounts of water critical. |
| Worms/intestinal parasites | Tansy (Tanacetum vulgare) tea, wild carrot leaves (poisonous), strong tannic acid | Use in moderation — all are toxic to humans too. |
| Gas/cramps | Carrot seed tea, mint leaf tea | Drink to settle stomach |
| Fungal infections | Walnut leaves, oak bark, acorns (decoction), broad-leaf plantain, jewelweed, vinegar | Apply frequently, alternating with direct sunlight. |
| Burns | Tannic acid, sugar, honey | See antiseptic section |
| Insect repellent | Garlic, onions (eat and rub juice on skin), sassafras leaves (rub on skin), cedar chips (around shelter) | |

**Tannic acid preparation:** All thready plants, especially trees, contain tannic acid. Hardwoods > softwoods. Oak (especially red and chestnut) contain the highest amount. Warty knots in oak can contain up to 28% tannic acid. Boil knots, inner bark, or pine needles (cut into 2cm strips). Boil 15 min (weak) to 2 hours (moderate) to 12 hours-3 days (very strong). Stronger concoctions are darker and more vile-tasting.
