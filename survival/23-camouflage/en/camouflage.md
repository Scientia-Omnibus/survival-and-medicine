> In a survival situation, you may need to camouflage yourself, your equipment, and your movement. Effective camouflage may mean the difference between survival and capture.

## Personal Camouflage

Certain shapes are particular to humans — hats, helmets, boots. Break up your outline with vegetation from the surrounding area. Reduce shine from skin or equipment. Blend in with surrounding colors and textures.

### Shape and Outline

Change the outline of weapons and equipment by tying vegetation or cloth strips without hindering operation. When hiding, cover yourself and equipment with local debris.

### Color and Texture by Area

| Area | Method |
|---|---|
| Temperate deciduous forest | Blotches |
| Coniferous forest | Broad slash |
| Jungle | Broad slash |
| Desert | Slash |
| Arctic | Blotches |
| Grass or open area | Slash |

Cover all exposed skin (face, hands, neck, ears). Use darker colors on protruding areas (forehead, nose, cheekbones, chin, ears). Use lighter colors on recessed areas (around eyes, under chin). Use irregular patterns. Replace wilting vegetation. Adjust colors as you move through different areas.

### Shine

- Wash oily skin and reapply camouflage frequently.
- Cover glass objects (mirrors, glasses, binoculars) when not in use.
- Camouflage glasses with a thin layer of dust on the outside of lenses.
- Cover shiny spots on equipment with paint, mud, cloth, or tape.
- Pay attention to boot eyelets, buckles, watches, jewelry, zippers, insignia.

### Shadow

- Stay in the deepest part of shadows.
- Keep vegetation between you and potential observers.
- Be aware of where your shadow falls, especially around buildings at night.
- In a dark shadow with a light source to one side, an observer on the other side can see your silhouette.

### Movement

- Move slowly to avoid detection and conserve energy.
- Avoid going over obstacles. If climbing, keep body level with the top.
- Do not silhouette against the skyline when crossing hills or ridges.
- Stop frequently, listen, and look around slowly.

### Noise

- Slow your pace to avoid making noise.
- Use background noises (aircraft, trucks, wind, rain, voices) to mask movement.
- Rain masks movement noise but reduces your ability to hear threats.

### Scent

| Measure | Method |
|---|---|
| Wash without soap | Removes body odors |
| Avoid strong foods | Garlic, tobacco, candy, gum, cosmetics |
| Use aromatic plants | Pine needles, mint on body and clothing |
| Smoke | Older smoke can mask scent from animals |
| Use wind | Approach from or skirt around on the downwind side |

## Methods of Stalking

| Method | Technique |
|---|---|
| Upright | Half-normal stride. Step with outside edge of ball of foot, roll inward, place heel, then toes. Shift weight forward. ~1 minute per step. |
| Crawling | Hands and knees. Move one limb at a time. Set down softly. |
| Prone | Low modified push-up. Move forward slightly, lower slowly. Avoid dragging. |
| Animal stalking | Use objects as concealment. Stop when animal looks your way. Squint eyes, close mouth. |

## Antitracking Techniques

| Technique | Description |
|---|---|
| Restore vegetation | Lift crushed vegetation with a stick |
| Brush out tracks | Use tree branch to brush or pat out tracks in open ground |
| Use hard or stony ground | Minimizes signs left |
| Abrupt direction changes | Combined with hard ground, slows visual trackers |
| Use well-used paths | Only if traffic patterns are known |
| Foot coverings | Sandbags, rags, old socks, sheepskin covers |
| Change footgear | Vary tread pattern on hard ground |
| Walk backwards | Avoid turning foot out and dragging dirt backward |
| Confuse start point | Walk cloverleaf patterns before departing on initial route |
| Use waterways | Only if moving in desired direction; more signs on exit |
| Cross roads with traffic | Blend into normal patterns instead of crossing perpendicular |
| Careful foot placement | Minimize heel and toe dig. Vary techniques. |

## Antidog Tracking

> You are trying to beat the handler, not the dog.

| Technique | Effect |
|---|---|
| Open ground in high wind | Wind blows scent; slows tracking team |
| Thick terrain, zigzag pattern | Slows and tires the handler |
| Hard or stony ground in wind/heat | Scent dissipates faster |
| Crowded places | Dog may lose track if not scent-specific trained |
| Freshly plowed or fertilized fields | Overpowering scents may confuse the dog |
| Maintain constant speed | Running increases scent from sweat and disturbance |
| Vehicle travel | Increases time and distance advantage |
