> Fish and mollusks may be a major food source. They present danger in three ways: attacking and biting, injecting venom through spines or tentacles, and toxic flesh when eaten.

## Fish That Attack Man

| Species | Description | Danger | First Aid |
|---|---|---|---|
| Shark | White, tiger, hammerhead, blue most dangerous. Size unrelated to attack likelihood. | Bite capable of maiming or killing. Schools of small sharks also dangerous. | Stop bleeding immediately. Get victim to raft or shore. In water, form circle around victim. |
| Barracuda | Found near reefs and shallow water. | Vicious bite. | Stop bleeding; treat wound. |
| Moray eel | Found near reefs. Aggressive when disturbed. | Vicious bite. | Stop bleeding; treat wound. |
| Piranha | 25-60 cm. Large teeth, travel in schools. Northern South America. | Can devour large animals in minutes. | Stop bleeding; treat wound. |

## Venomous Fish and Invertebrates

| Species | Description | Habitat | Danger |
|---|---|---|---|
| Stingray | Distinctive ray shape. Venomous barbed spines in tail. | Shallow water, tropics and temperate. | Severe or fatal injury. |
| Rabbitfish | ~30 cm. Sharp venomous spines in fins. | Reefs, Pacific and Indian oceans. | Intense pain. |
| Scorpion fish | 30-90 cm. Reddish, long wavy fins and spines. | Reefs, Pacific and Indian oceans. | Intensely painful sting. |
| Siganus fish | 10-15 cm. Looks like small tuna. Venomous spines. | Tropical waters. | Painful stings. |
| Stonefish | ~30 cm. Subdued colors, lumpy camouflage. | Tropical waters, Pacific and Indian oceans. | Extremely painful and sometimes fatal wound when stepped on. |
| Tang / surgeonfish | 20-25 cm. Needlelike spines on side of tail. | All tropical waters. | Extremely painful wounds. |
| Toadfish | 17.5-25 cm. Dull color, large mouth. Buries in sand. | Tropical waters off South and Central America. | Very sharp, extremely poisonous dorsal spines. |
| Weever fish | ~30 cm. Slim. All fins have venomous spines. | Tropical waters. | Painful wound. |
| Blue-ringed octopus | Small, grayish-white with iridescent blue rings. | Great Barrier Reef, Australia. | Bite extremely poisonous, frequently lethal. |
| Portuguese man-of-war | Floating colony. Tentacles up to 12 m. | Tropical regions. | Painful, incapacitating sting; rarely fatal. |
| Cone shells | Colorful mottled shell. Tiny needlelike teeth. | Under rocks, crevices, coral reefs. Tropical. | Extremely poisonous venom: acute pain, swelling, paralysis, blindness, possible death within hours. |
| Terebra shells | Similar to cone shells but thinner and longer. | Temperate and tropical waters. | Similar to cone shells but less poisonous. |

> **First aid for venomous injuries:** Treat as for snakebite. Immobilize affected area. Soak wound in hot water (as hot as victim can tolerate) for 30-90 minutes to denature venom. Seek medical aid.

## Fish with Toxic Flesh

No simple rules distinguish edible fish from those with poisonous flesh. Common characteristics of toxic fish:
- Live in shallow water around reefs or lagoons
- Boxy or round bodies with hard shell-like skin, bony plates or spines
- Small parrotlike mouths, small gills, small or absent belly fins

### Precautions

| Situation | Action |
|---|---|
| Shallow lagoons with sandy or coral bottom | Be very careful — reef-feeding species may be poisonous |
| Leeward side of an island | Avoid — shallow water with living corals may contain poisonous fish |
| Discolored water | Do not eat fish from discolored water (possible plankton toxicity) |
| Windward side or deep passages | Safer fishing — deepwater fish are usually not poisonous |
| Suspected reef fish | Discard regardless of where caught |
