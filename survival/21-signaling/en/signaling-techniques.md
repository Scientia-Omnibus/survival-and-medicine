> One of your first concerns when you find yourself in a survival situation is to communicate with your friends or allies. Generally, communication is the giving and receiving of information. In a survival situation, you must first get your rescuer's attention, then second, send a message your rescuer understands. Some attention-getters are man-made geometric patterns such as straight lines, circles, triangles, or Xs displayed in uninhabited areas; a large fire or flash of light; a large, bright object moving slowly; or contrast, whether from color or shadows. The type of signal used will depend on your environment and the enemy situation.

## APPLICATION

| Situation | Approach |
|---|---|
| Noncombat | Find the largest clear, flat area on the highest terrain possible. Create the most obvious signal possible. |
| Combat | Choose area visible from air but with hiding places nearby. Keep hill or object between signal site and enemy to mask signal. Reconnoiter for enemy forces. |

A radio is the surest and quickest way to communicate. Learn how to operate unit radios before you need them.

## MEANS FOR SIGNALING

### Visual Signals

| Signal | Method | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| Fire | Three fires in triangle or straight line (~25m apart). Maintain hot coal bed for infrared detection. | Nighttime |
| Tree torch | Set pitch-bearing trees afire. Add green trees for more smoke. | Night or day |
| Smoke | Three columns of smoke. White = green leaves/moss/water smothered on fire. Black = rubber/oil-soaked rags. | Daylight, calm clear days |
| Smoke grenades | Same pattern as fires. Red is international distress color. Keep dry. | Daylight |
| Pen flare (M185) | Fires flare ~150m high. Cock, aim clear of obstructions. Sounds like pistol shot. | Day or night |
| Gyro-jet | Jet-powered, up to 300m height. Push into crimped collar. Better canopy penetration. | Day or night |
| Tracer ammunition | Fire rifle/pistol tracers to signal. Do not fire in front of aircraft. | Night |
| Star clusters | Reach 200–215m, burn 6–10 seconds. Red is international distress color. | Night |
| Star parachute flares | Reach 200–215m, descend at 2.1m/s. M126 (red) burns ~50s, M127 (white) ~25s. Visible 48–56km at night. | Night |
| MK-13 / MK-124 | Orange smoke (day) and flare (night). Smoke ~15s, flare 20–25s. Do not discard until both ends used. | Day and night |
| Mirror / shiny objects | Best sunny-day signal. Direct flashes. Visible up to 160km under ideal conditions. | Sunny daylight |
| Flashlight / strobe | SOS signal at night. Strobe flashes 60 times/min. | Night |
| Laser devices | Highly visible. Targeting pointers, presentation pointers. | Night |
| Firefly lights | ~3cm square, snap onto 9V battery. Blinking and steady versions available. Infrared models exist. | Night |
| VS-17 panel | Orange side up (more visible from air than violet). Flash to attract attention. | Daylight |
| Clothing | Spread contrasting-colored clothing in large geometric pattern on ground or in tree. | Daylight |
| Natural materials | Mounds casting shadows; brush, rocks, snow blocks. Tramp snow to form letters and fill with contrasting material. | Daylight |
| Sea dye markers | Visible >11km from aircraft at 2,000ft. Slick ~30m. Conserve — dip bag, do not use all at once. Also effective on snow. | Daylight |

> **Groups of threes** are internationally recognized distress signals because nature does not normally replicate anything in groups of three.

### Audio Signals

| Signal | Range / Method | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Radio (AN/PRC-90, AN/PRC-112) | Varies by altitude, terrain, vegetation, weather, battery. | Line-of-sight. Transmit from clear, unobstructed terrain. Keep antenna at right angles to aircraft. |
| Whistle | Heard up to 1.6km. | Excellent for close-up signaling. |
| Gunshots | Three shots at distinct intervals = distress. | Do not use in enemy territory. |

## CODES AND SIGNALS

### SOS

Three dots, three dashes, three dots (repeating). Use lights or flags. Dots = short pulse. Dashes = longer pulse. Flags: left side for dashes, right side for dots.

### Ground-to-Air Emergency Code

| Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| V | Require assistance |
| X | Require medical assistance |
| → (single direction arrow) | Proceed in this direction |
| Y | Yes / Affirmative |
| N | No / Negative |
| LL (two long lines) | Require medical aid (alternate) |

> Symbols should be minimum 4m wide × 6m long (2:3 ratio). Signal arms/legs: 1m wide × 1m high. Ensure strong contrast with background. Orient north-south for maximum sun shadow.

### Body Signals

| Position | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Arms above head, waving | Pick us up / Need assistance |
| One arm raised | Need help |
| Arms extended horizontally | Need mechanical help / Landing direction |
| Arms extended, fists clenched | Short delay / Can proceed shortly |
| Hands on head | Do not attempt to land / Have help |
| Sitting on ground, arms out | Need medical assistance / OK to land here |

### Panel Signals

Use life raft cover, sail, space blanket, or combat casualty blanket. Arrange in large geometric patterns visible from the air.

## AIRCRAFT VECTORING PROCEDURES

### Initial Contact (Radio)

| Period | Duration | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Beacon | 15 seconds | Transmit beacon tone. |
| Voice | 15 seconds | "Mayday, Mayday, Mayday — this is [call sign]" |
| Listen | 15 seconds | Wait for response. |

### Guiding Aircraft to Your Location

| Step | Information to Transmit |
|---|---|
| 1 | Call sign (if any) |
| 2 | Your name |
| 3 | Location (clock direction and distance from aircraft) |
| 4 | Enemy disposition and location |
| 5 | Number of people needing rescue |
| 6 | Available landing sites |
| 7 | Remarks (medical aid, other needs) |
| 8 | Steering corrections from pilot's perspective: "steer left," "roll out," distance countdown |

### Aircraft Acknowledgments

| Signal | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Flying low, moving plane, flashing lights | Pilot has sighted you. |
| Wing waggle | Message received and understood. |
| Circle back | Message received but not understood. |
