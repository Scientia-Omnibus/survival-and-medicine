> One of man's most urgent requirements is food. In contemplating virtually any hypothetical survival situation, the mind immediately turns to thoughts of food. Unless the situation occurs in an arid environment, even water, which is more important to maintaining body functions, will usually follow food in our initial thoughts. The survivor must remember that the three essentials of survival — water, food, and shelter — are prioritized according to the estimate of the actual situation. This estimate must not only be timely but accurate as well. We can live for weeks without food but it may take days or weeks to determine what is safe to eat and to trap animals in the area. Therefore, you need to begin food gathering in the earliest stages of survival as your endurance will decrease daily. Some situations may well dictate that shelter precede both food and water.

### ANIMALS FOR FOOD

Concentrate your efforts on smaller animals — they are more abundant and easier to prepare. Relatively few animals are poisonous. Learn the habits and behavioral patterns of classes of animals: those that are excellent for trapping, inhabit a particular range, occupy a den or nest, have fixed feeding areas, and have trails. Larger herding animals (elk, caribou) roam vast areas and are harder to trap.

With few exceptions, you can eat anything that crawls, swims, walks, or flies. Overcome any natural aversion. Thoroughly cook all food sources whenever possible.

**INSECTS**

The most abundant and easily caught life-form. Many insects provide 65-80% protein (beef: 20%).

| Edible Insects | Where to Find | Preparation |
|---|---|---|
| Ants, termites, beetles, grubs | Under rotting logs, in nests on/in ground | Remove wings and barbed legs. Cook hard-shelled (beetles, grasshoppers) to kill parasites. Soft-shelled can be eaten raw. |
| Insect larvae | Grassy areas, under stones/boards | Can be eaten raw |

Avoid: adults that sting or bite, hairy or brightly colored insects, caterpillars, insects with pungent odor, spiders, ticks, flies, mosquitoes.

**WORMS**

Excellent protein source. Dig in damp humus soil, rootballs of grass clumps, or collect after rain. Place in clean water for 15 minutes to purge, then eat raw.

**CRUSTACEANS**

| Type | Where to Find | How to Catch |
|---|---|---|
| Freshwater shrimp | Floating algae mats, mud bottoms of ponds/lakes, 0.25-2.5cm | Net or scoop |
| Crayfish | Under stones in streams, soft mud near chimney-like nest holes | Tie bait (offal/internal organs) to string. Pull to shore when they grab. |
| Saltwater lobsters, crabs, shrimp | Surf line to 10m depth | Baited trap or hook (lobsters/crabs), net (shrimp come to light at night) |

> **CAUTION: All freshwater crustaceans, mollusks, and fish must be cooked — freshwater harbors dangerous organisms and pollutants.**

**MOLLUSKS**

Includes snails, clams, mussels, bivalves, barnacles, periwinkles, chitons, sea urchins, octopuses.

| Environment | Where to Find |
|---|---|
| Freshwater | Shallows with sandy/muddy bottom. Look for narrow trails in mud or dark elliptical slit of open valves. |
| Seashore | Tidal pools, wet sand. Rocks near beaches or reefs. Snails/limpets cling from low water mark upward. Chitons adhere above surf line. |
| Mussels | Dense colonies in rock pools, on logs, at base of boulders |

Steam, boil, or bake in shell. Make excellent stews with greens and tubers.

> **CAUTION: Mussels may be poisonous in tropical zones during summer. If red tide occurred within 72 hours, do not eat fish or shellfish from that water. Do not eat shellfish not covered by water at high tide.**

**FISH**

Good source of protein and fat. Usually more abundant than mammals. Methods of catching are silent.

| Fish Behavior | Best Fishing Times/Locations |
|---|---|
| Feed heavily | Before a storm |
| Rest | Near rocks in eddies (heavy current), deep pools, under overhanging brush, near submerged foliage/logs |
| Attracted to light | At night |

No poisonous freshwater fish, but cook all freshwater fish to kill parasites. The barbel has poisonous roe. Catfish have sharp dorsal protrusions. Marine fish caught within a reef or near freshwater sources should be cooked. Open-ocean fish can be eaten raw.

Poisonous marine fish (ciguatera risk): barracudas, jacks, mackerel, triggerfish, snappers, groupers. Cooking, drying, smoking, or marinating does not eliminate ciguatera toxins. Other poisonous fish: porcupine fish, cowfish, thorn fish, oilfish, puffer.

**AMPHIBIANS**

| Type | Characteristics | Edibility |
|---|---|---|
| Frogs | Smooth moist skin, found near fresh water | Edible. Avoid brightly colored frogs, those with "X" on back, tree frogs. |
| Toads | Dry warty/bumpy skin, found on land | Not edible. Secrete poisonous substance through skin. |
| Salamanders | Smooth moist skin, 4 toes per foot, near water | Do not eat — only ~25% are edible, not worth the risk. |

**REPTILES**

Good protein source, relatively easy to catch. Thorough cooking and hand washing is imperative — all reptiles carry salmonella.

| Type | Notes |
|---|---|
| Lizards | Dry scaly skin, 5 toes per foot. Avoid Gila monster and Mexican beaded lizard (poisonous). Iguana and monitor lizard often carry salmonella in mouth/teeth. Tail meat is best. |
| Turtles | Very good meat source. Box turtle feeds on poisonous mushrooms (cooking doesn't destroy toxin). Avoid hawksbill turtle (poisonous thorax gland). |
| Snakes | Cook same as small game. Poisonous snakes, alligators, crocodiles, large sea turtles present obvious hazards. |

**BIRDS**

All species edible, though flavor varies. Only poisonous bird: Pitohui (New Guinea). Skin fish-eating birds to improve taste.

| Bird Type | Nesting Places | Nesting Period |
|---|---|---|
| Inland birds | Trees, woods, fields | Spring/early summer (temperate/arctic); year-round (tropics) |
| Cranes and herons | Mangrove swamps, high trees near water | Spring and early summer |
| Some owls | High trees | Late December through March |
| Ducks, geese, swans | Tundra near ponds, rivers, lakes | Spring and early summer (arctic) |
| Some sea birds | Sandbars, low sand islands | Spring and early summer (temperate/arctic) |
| Gulls, auks, murres, cormorants | Steep rocky coasts | Spring and early summer (temperate/arctic) |

Nesting birds also provide eggs. Remove all but 2-3 eggs from clutch, mark the ones you leave. The bird will lay more to fill the clutch.

**MAMMALS**

Excellent protein source. All mammals are edible. Exceptions: polar bear and bearded seal livers (toxic vitamin A), platypus (poisonous hind claws). Scavenging mammals (opossum) may carry diseases. All mammals will bite in self-defense.

### TRAPS AND SNARES

For an unarmed survivor or when silence is needed. To be effective:

- Know the species you intend to catch.
- Construct proper traps and mask your scent.
- Do not alarm prey with signs of your presence.

**Signs of animal presence:**

| Sign | What to Look For |
|---|---|
| Runs and trails | Distinct paths (trails = multiple species; runs = single species) |
| Tracks | Footprints in soft ground |
| Droppings | Scat size and shape indicates species |
| Chewed/rubbed vegetation | Feeding signs |
| Nesting/roosting sites | Shelters and beds |
| Feeding and watering areas | Regular visit locations |

**Scent masking:**

| Method | Technique |
|---|---|
| Gall/urine fluid | Use fluid from previous kills' gall and urine bladders. Do not use human urine. |
| Mud | Use mud from areas with rotting vegetation to coat hands and trap parts |
| Smoke | Smoke trap parts over a fire |
| Weathering | Allow trap to weather for a few days before setting; do not handle during weathering |

**TRAP AND SNARE TYPES**

| Trap | Mechanism | Prey | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Simple Snare | Noose tightens around neck | Small game on trails | Wire is best. Use twigs/grass/spider webs to hold noose open. Not always lethal. |
| Drag Noose | Noose attached to crossmember on forked sticks; animal drags it | Animals on runs | Crossmember catches on vegetation, entangling the animal. |
| Twitch-Up Snare | Bent sapling springs up when trigger released | Small game | Uses forked sticks as trigger. Do not use green sticks for trigger (sap glues them). |
| Squirrel Pole | Wire nooses on pole against tree | Squirrels | Nooses 5-6cm diameter, ~2.5cm off pole. Top/bottom nooses 45cm from ends. |
| Ojibwa Bird Pole | Perch triggers slip noose with weight | Birds | Pole in open area. Cord through hole with weight. Bird lands on perch, releasing weight that tightens noose. |
| Noosing Wand | Slip noose on long pole | Roosting birds, small mammals | Requires patient operator. Slip noose over neck and pull tight. |
| Treadle Spring Snare | Foot pressure on covered hole releases trigger | Small game on trails | Dig shallow hole, cover with sticks, set noose above. Use channelization. |
| Figure 4 Deadfall | Notched sticks hold weight; prey disturbs bait | Small game | Weight crushes prey. Requires precise notches and angles. |
| Paiute Deadfall | Cordage and catch stick hold weight | Small game | Easier to set than figure 4. Bait stick releases catch stick. |
| Bow Trap | Trip wire releases bow | Medium game | Lethal. Approach from rear only. Arrow fired when trip wire pulled. |
| Pig Spear Shaft | Trip wire releases sprung spear shaft | Medium/large game | Lethal. Spear impales prey against tree. |
| Bottle Trap | Pit with narrow top, wide bottom | Mice, voles | Covered with bark/wood on small stones. Prey falls in and can't climb out. Check for snakes. |

> **WARNING: Bow traps and pig spear shafts are lethal. Approach from the rear only!**

### KILLING DEVICES

| Device | Description | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| Rabbit stick | Stout stick, fingertip-to-shoulder length; thrown sidearm | Small game that freezes as defense |
| Spear | 1.2-1.5m shaft, sharpened or bladed; jab, do not throw | Small game and fish |
| Bow and arrow | Hardwood bow ~1m, arrows ~half bow length | Small to medium game |
| Sling | Two cords (~60cm) on palm-sized leather/cloth | Small game; requires practice |

**Bow construction:** Select hardwood stick ~1m, free of knots. Scrape large end down to match pull of small end. Follow natural curve; scrape from facing side. Dead dry wood is best. To increase pull, lash a second bow front-to-front forming an X.

**Arrow construction:** Use straightest dry sticks ~half bow length. Straighten by heating over coals. Notch ends for bowstring (cut or file, do not split). Fletching improves flight but is optional.

### FISHING DEVICES

| Device | Description |
|---|---|
| Improvised fishhooks | From pins, needles, wire, nails, wood, bone, coconut shell, thorns, flint, seashell |
| Gorge/skewer | Small shaft sharp on both ends, notched in middle. Bait lengthwise. Let fish swallow before setting. |
| Stakeout | Two supple saplings driven into water bottom. Cord tied between them just below surface. Hooks attached. |
| Gill net | Made from parachute suspension line. Mesh ~2.5cm square. Angle to reduce debris. |
| Fish traps | Funnel-shaped baskets of lashed sticks, or stone walls at tide lines |
| Spearfishing | Long straight sapling, sharpened or bladed. Push, don't throw. Aim lower than fish appears (light refraction). |
| Chop fishing | Use light to attract fish at night; strike with back of blade (not sharp side). |
| Fish poison | Use rotenone-bearing plants in ponds or headwaters. Works fast in water 21°C+; slow in 10-21°C; ineffective below 10°C. |

**Fish poison plants:**

| Plant | Region | How to Use |
|---|---|---|
| Anamirta cocculus | Southern Asia, South Pacific | Crush bean-shaped seeds, throw in water |
| Croton tiglium | South Pacific islands | Crush seeds, throw in water |
| Barringtonia | Malaya, Polynesia | Crush seeds and bark, throw in water |
| Derris eliptica | Tropics (main commercial rotenone source) | Grind roots to powder, mix with water, throw large quantity in |
| Duboisia | Australia | Crush whole plants, throw in water |
| Tephrosia | Tropics worldwide | Crush/bruise bundles of leaves and stems, throw in water |
| Lime (calcium oxide) | Commercial or from burning coral/seashells | Throw into water |
| Nut husks | Butternuts or black walnuts | Crush green husks, throw in water |

### COOKING AND STORAGE OF FISH AND GAME

**FISH — Signs of spoilage:**

- Sunken eyes.
- Peculiar odor.
- Suspicious color (gills should be red/pink; scales gray, not faded).
- Dents remain after pressing with thumb.
- Slimy body (should be moist/wet).
- Sharp or peppery taste.

Symptoms of spoiled fish (1-6 hours after eating): diarrhea, nausea, cramps, vomiting, itching, paralysis, metallic taste. Induce vomiting if symptoms appear.

| Preparation | Method |
|---|---|
| Impale whole on stick | Cook over open fire |
| Boil with skin on | Best food value — fats/oils under skin; save juices for broth |
| Clay pack | Pack in clay ball, bury in coals until clay hardens; meat flakes off when done |
| Smoking | Cut off head, remove backbone. Smoke for preservation. |

**SNAKES:** Cut off head (including 10-15cm behind head to remove venom sac). Bury sac. Slit skin down body, peel back, pull apart. Remove entrails. Cut into sections, boil or roast.

**BIRDS:** Pluck or skin. Remove entrails, save craw (seed-eating birds), heart, liver. Cut off feet. Boil or roast over spit. Boil scavenger birds at least 20 minutes.

**SKINNING AND BUTCHERING GAME**

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Bleed by cutting throat. Clean near stream if possible. |
| 2 | Split hide from throat to tail (belly up), cutting around sexual organs. Remove musk glands. |
| 3 | Insert knife blade under skin, turn blade up to cut hide only (avoids cutting hair onto meat). |
| 4 | Remove entrails. For small game: split body, pull out with fingers. For large game: cut gullet from diaphragm, roll out entrails. |
| 5 | Cut around anus, reach into lower abdomen, grasp lower intestine, pull to remove. |
| 6 | Pinch off urine bladder, cut below fingers. Wash meat if urine spills. |
| 7 | Save heart and liver. Inspect for worms/parasites. Liver should be smooth, wet, deep red/purple. Discard if diseased. |
| 8 | Cut hide from above each foot to body cut. Pull hide away, cutting connective tissue. |
| 9 | Cut off head and feet. Cut larger game into manageable pieces. |

**Cutting larger game:**

| Cut | Method |
|---|---|
| Front legs | Slice muscle tissue connecting to body (no joints connecting front legs to body) |
| Hindquarters | Cut around large bone at top of leg, cut to ball-and-socket joint, cut ligaments, bend back to separate |
| Backstrap | Remove large muscles (tenderloin) on either side of spine |
| Ribs | Separate from backbone. Break ribs first, then cut through breaks. |

**Cooking:** Boil large pieces or cook over spit. Stew/boil smaller pieces as soup. Organs (heart, liver, pancreas, spleen, kidneys, brain) can be cooked same as muscle meat. Tongue: skin, boil until tender.

**SMOKING MEAT**

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Prepare enclosure around fire (two ponchos snapped together works) |
| 2 | Use low heat/smoke, not flame. Use hardwoods (not resinous). Wood should be somewhat green; soak if too dry. |
| 3 | Cut meat into thin slices, no more than 6mm thick |
| 4 | Drape slices over framework; ensure none touch each other |
| 5 | Keep enclosure around meat to hold smoke; watch fire closely |
| 6 | Smoke overnight → lasts ~1 week. Two days continuous smoking → lasts 2-4 weeks. |

Properly smoked meat looks like a dark, curled, brittle stick. Can eat without further cooking.

**DRYING MEAT:** Cut into 6mm strips with the grain. Hang on rack in sunny location with good airflow. Keep out of animal reach. Cover to keep off blowflies. Properly dried meat is dry, crisp, and not cool to touch.

**OTHER PRESERVATION METHODS:** Freezing (cold climates) — indefinitely, but must still cook before eating. Brine/salt — soak meat thoroughly in saltwater solution, or use dry salt (wash off before cooking).
