> To survive and evade in arid or desert areas, you must understand and prepare for the environment you will face. You must determine your equipment needs, the tactics you will use, and how the environment will affect you and your tactics. Your survival will depend upon your knowledge of the terrain, basic climatic elements, your ability to cope with these elements, and your will to survive.

## TERRAIN

Most arid areas have several types of terrain.

### Desert Terrain Types

| Type | Description | Hazards / Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Mountainous (high altitude) | Scattered barren hills or mountains separated by dry, flat basins. High ground may rise gradually or abruptly. | Flash floods erode deep gullies and ravines. |
| Rocky plateau | Slight relief, extensive flat areas with solid or broken rock. Steep-walled eroded valleys (wadis, arroyos, canyons). | Flash flooding in narrow valleys. |
| Sand dunes | Extensive flat areas of sand or gravel. Dunes can exceed 300m high and 16–24km long. | Trafficability depends on slope and sand texture. |
| Salt marshes | Flat, desolate with alkali salt deposits. Crust 2.5–30cm thick over saltwater. | Undrinkable water, biting insects, corrosive to boots/clothing/skin. Avoid. |
| Broken terrain | Highly dissected terrain formed by rain erosion. Wadis form mazelike patterns. | Difficult to negotiate but provides cover and concealment. |

Desert terrain makes movement difficult and demanding. Land navigation is extremely difficult with few landmarks. Cover and concealment may be very limited.

## ENVIRONMENTAL FACTORS

### Seven Environmental Factors

| Factor | Description | Impact on Survivor |
|---|---|---|
| Low rainfall | Less than 10cm (4in) annually. Rain comes in brief torrents. | Water is primary concern. First priority: locate and conserve water. |
| Intense sunlight and heat | Air temps up to 60°C (140°F). Sand temps 16–22°C hotter than air. | Greatly increases water need. Build shelter to reduce exposure. Travel at night. |
| Wide temperature range | Day: up to 55°C (130°F). Night: as low as 10°C (50°F). | Need warm clothing for night. Cool evenings best for work or travel. |
| Sparse vegetation | Little plant life for cover or concealment. | Large areas visible in daylight. Use wadis and shadows. |
| High mineral content | Soil contains borax, salt, alkali, lime. | Water undrinkable. Material wears out quickly. Skin rash possible. |
| Sandstorms | Sand-laden winds. Major storms at least weekly. Winds 3–128 kph. | Risk of getting lost. Wear goggles, cover mouth/nose. Radio interference. |
| Mirages | Optical refraction of light through heated air. | Obscures natural features. Get to high ground (3m+) to overcome effect. |

Radios and sensitive equipment exposed to direct sunlight will malfunction.

### Water Requirements

| Activity Level | Air Temp | Daily Water Need |
|---|---|---|
| Hard work in sun | 43°C (109°F) | 19 liters (5 gallons) |

Body temperature is normally 36.9°C (98.6°F). The body cools by sweating. If you stop sweating during high heat and heavy exertion, heat stroke follows immediately.

### Water Conservation

| Measure | How To |
|---|---|
| Find shade | Get out of the sun. |
| Insulate from ground | Place something between you and hot ground. |
| Limit movements | Reduce physical activity in heat of day. |
| Conserve sweat | Wear complete uniform, sleeves down, head covered, neck protected. Stay quiet, mouth closed, breathe through nose. |
| Do not eat if water scarce | Food requires water for digestion. |

### Drinking Schedule

| Temp | Drink |
|---|---|
| Below 38°C (100°F) | 0.5 liter every hour |
| Above 38°C (100°F) | 1 liter every hour |

> **Do not ration water.** Sipping water constantly keeps the body cooler and reduces sweating. Thirst is not reliable — a person using thirst as a guide drinks only two-thirds of daily requirement.

### Heat Casualties

| Type | Cause | Symptoms | Treatment |
|---|---|---|---|
| Heat cramps | Loss of salt from excessive sweating | Muscle cramps in legs, arms, or abdomen | Stop activity. Get in shade. Drink water. |
| Heat exhaustion | Large loss of water and salt | Headache, confusion, irritability, excessive sweating, weakness, dizziness, cramps, pale clammy skin | Lie on stretcher 45cm off ground in shade. Loosen clothing. Sprinkle with water and fan. Drink small amounts of water every 3 minutes. |
| Heat stroke | Extreme water/salt loss; body cannot cool | No sweat, hot dry skin, headache, dizziness, fast pulse, nausea, unconsciousness | Immediate cooling. Pour water on body, fan. Massage limbs. Small amounts of water every 3 minutes if conscious. |

### Precautions

| Precaution | Details |
|---|---|
| Inform others | Tell someone where you are going and when you will return. |
| Watch for heat injury | Complaints of tiredness or wandering off may indicate heat casualty. |
| Hydrate regularly | Drink water at least once an hour. |
| Rest in shade | Do not lie directly on the ground. |
| Keep shirt on | Do not remove shirt and work during the day. |
| Check urine color | Light = adequate water. Dark = need more. |

## DESERT HAZARDS

| Hazard | Description | Precautions |
|---|---|---|
| Insects | Lice, mites, wasps, flies. Found in old buildings, ruins, caves. | Wear gloves. Inspect area before sitting. Shake out boots and clothing. |
| Snakes | Inhabit ruins, villages, garbage dumps, caves, rock outcroppings. | Never go barefoot. Inspect area. Watch hands and feet. Give wide berth. |
| Thorned plants and cacti | Present in most desert terrain. | Handle with care. Wear protective clothing. |
| Contaminated water | May be polluted or mineral-laden. | Purify all water before drinking. |
| Sunburn | Intense sunlight. | Cover skin, wear head covering. |
| Eye irritation | Sand, dust, glare. | Wear goggles or improvised eye protection. |
| Climatic stress | Heat, dryness, temperature swings. | Rest during day, work at night. Stay hydrated. |
