> In many survival situations, the ability to start a fire can make the difference between living and dying. Fire can fulfill many needs. It can provide warmth and comfort. It not only cooks and preserves food, it also provides warmth in the form of heated food that saves calories our body normally uses to produce body heat. You can use fire to purify water, sterilize bandages, signal for rescue, and provide protection from animals. It can be a psychological boost by providing peace of mind and companionship. You can also use fire to produce tools and weapons.
>
> Fire can cause problems, as well. The enemy can detect the smoke and light it produces. It can cause forest fires or destroy essential equipment. Fire can also cause burns and carbon monoxide poisoning when used in shelters.
>
> Weigh your need for fire against your need to avoid enemy detection.

### BASIC FIRE PRINCIPLES

Fuel (in a nongaseous state) does not burn directly. When you apply heat to a fuel, it produces a gas. This gas, combined with oxygen in the air, burns. The fire triangle represents **air, heat, and fuel**. Remove any of these and the fire goes out. The correct ratio of these components is critical.

### SITE SELECTION AND PREPARATION

Before building a fire, consider: the area (terrain and climate), materials and tools available, time, need, and security.

Look for a dry spot that:

- Is protected from the wind.
- Is suitably placed in relation to your shelter.
- Will concentrate heat in the direction you desire.
- Has a supply of wood or other fuel.

Clear a circle at least 1m in diameter. If time allows, construct a fire wall using logs or rocks to reflect/direct heat.

> **CAUTION: Do not use wet or porous rocks — they may explode when heated.**

**Underground fireplace (Dakota fire hole):**

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Dig a hole in the ground |
| 2 | On the upwind side, dig a connecting hole for ventilation |
| 3 | Build your fire in the hole |

In snow-covered areas: use green logs to make a dry base. Cut/break several wrist-sized green logs, lay them side by side on the snow. Add 1-2 more layers with the top layer opposite the one below.

### HOW TO BUILD A FIRE

| Method | Description |
|---|---|
| **Tepee** | Arrange tinder and kindling in a cone shape. Light center. As it burns, outside logs fall inward. Burns well even with wet wood. |
| **Lean-To** | Push a green stick into ground at 30-degree angle, pointing into wind. Place tinder under it. Lean kindling against it. Light tinder. |
| **Cross-Ditch** | Scratch a 30cm cross in ground, 7.5cm deep. Put tinder wad in center. Build kindling pyramid above. Ditch provides draft. |
| **Pyramid** | Place two small logs parallel on ground. Layer smaller logs across them at right angles, each layer smaller. Make starter fire on top. Burns downward overnight without attention. |

---

### HOW TO LIGHT A FIRE

Always light from the upwind side.

**MODERN METHODS**

| Method | Materials | Technique |
|---|---|---|
| Matches | Waterproof matches, striker pad | Store in waterproof container |
| Convex lens | Binoculars, camera lens, magnifying glass | Angle to concentrate sun's rays on tinder. Hold over same spot until tinder smolders. Sunny days only. |
| Metal match | Metal match, knife | Place flat dry leaf under tinder. Scrape knife against metal match to produce sparks onto tinder. |
| Battery | Battery, wire | Attach wire to each terminal, touch bare wire ends together next to tinder. Steel wool ignites if touching both terminals. |
| Gunpowder | Ammunition | Carefully extract bullet from casing. Use gunpowder as tinder. A spark ignites it. |

> **WARNING: Primer caps remain sensitive. Even a small pile of gunpowder can give surprising results.**

**PRIMITIVE METHODS**

| Method | Materials | Technique |
|---|---|---|
| Flint and steel | Flint/chert/ hard rock, carbon steel (not stainless) | Strike rock with steel to produce sparks onto tinder. Requires loose wrist and practice. |
| Fire-plow | Softwood base, hardwood shaft | Cut groove in softwood. Plow blunt tip of hardwood shaft up and down groove. Friction ignites wood particles. |
| Bow and drill | Socket (stone/hardwood with depression), drill (straight seasoned hardwood ~2cm x 25cm), fire board (softwood ~2.5cm thick), bow (resilient green stick ~2.5cm), bowstring | Place tinder under V-cut in fire board. Loop bowstring over drill. Place socket on top of drill. Saw bow back and forth while pressing down. Hot black powder ignites tinder. |

**Primitive fire-making hints:**

- Use nonaromatic seasoned hardwood for fuel if possible.
- Collect kindling and tinder along the trail.
- Add insect repellent to tinder.
- Keep firewood dry; dry damp wood near the fire.
- Bank the fire to keep coals alive overnight.
- Carry lighted punk when possible.
- Be sure the fire is out before leaving camp.
- Do not select wood lying on the ground — it appears dry but doesn't provide enough friction.
