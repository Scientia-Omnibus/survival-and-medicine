> The threat from animals is less than from other parts of the environment. However, common sense tells you to avoid encounters with lions, bears, and other large or dangerous animals. You should also avoid large grazing animals with horns, hooves, and great weight. Move carefully through their environment. Do not attract large predators by leaving food lying around your camp. Carefully survey the scene before entering water or forests.
>
> Smaller animals actually present more of a threat to you than large animals. To compensate for their size, nature has given many small animals weapons such as fangs and stingers to defend themselves. Each year, a few people are bitten by sharks, mauled by alligators, and attacked by bears. Most of these incidents were in some way the victim's fault. However, each year more victims die from bites by relatively small venomous snakes than by large dangerous animals. Even more victims die from allergic reactions to bee stings. These smaller animals are the ones you are more likely to meet as you unwittingly move into their habitat, or they slip into your environment unnoticed.
>
> Keeping a level head and an awareness of your surroundings will keep you alive if you use a few simple safety procedures. Do not let curiosity and carelessness kill or injure you.

### INSECTS AND OTHER ARTHROPODS

| Group | Characteristics | Danger | Precautions |
|---|---|---|---|
| Scorpions | Nocturnal; 2.5cm avg (up to 20cm giants); brown/black in moist areas, yellow/light green in desert | Stings rarely fatal (children, elderly, ill at risk) | Check footgear, clothing, bedding daily. Use care turning rocks/logs. |
| Spiders | Brown recluse (violin mark), black widow (hourglass mark), funnelwebs, tarantulas | Bites vary from painful tissue degeneration to potentially deadly | Avoid dark places. Shake out clothing and boots. |
| Centipedes/millipedes | Mostly small; tropical/desert species up to 25cm | Some poisonous bites; greatest danger is infection | Brush off in direction they travel to avoid skin punctures. |
| Bees, wasps, hornets | Bees: hairy, thick body. Wasps/hornets: slender, nearly hairless. | Stings: barbed (bees, die after stinging) or smooth (wasps, sting repeatedly). Allergic reactions can cause anaphylaxis. | Watch for flowers/fruit where bees feed. Be careful of meat-eating yellow jackets when cleaning fish/game. |
| Ticks | Small round arachnids, soft or hard body | Spread Lyme disease, Rocky Mountain spotted fever, encephalitis. Requires ~6 hrs attached to transmit. | Inspect body thoroughly. Use insect repellent. Beware in thick vegetation and when cleaning host animals. |

### LEECHES

Bloodsucking wormlike creatures found in tropics and temperate zones. Encountered when swimming in infested waters, crossing water, or passing through swampy vegetation.

| Hazard | Prevention |
|---|---|
| Swallowed leeches attach to throat/nose | Treat questionable water by boiling or chemical treatment |
| Wounds from attached leeches | Keep trousers tucked in boots; check frequently; avoid camping in their habitats |

---

### BATS

Found worldwide. Vampire bats only in Central and South America.

| Hazard | Precautions |
|---|---|
| All bats considered rabies carriers | Any physical contact is a rabies risk |
| Bat dung (guano) in caves carries disease organisms | Avoid inhaling powdered guano |
| Thoroughly cooked flying foxes/bats are safe to eat | Emphasis on thorough cooking |

### VENOMOUS SNAKES

The best strategy is to leave all snakes alone. Where venomous species are plentiful, the risk of bites negates their food value.

**Safety rules in snake country:**

| Rule | Action |
|---|---|
| Watch where you step | Step onto logs rather than over them. During evasion, step over or go around logs. |
| Look when picking fruit or moving around water | |
| Do not molest snakes | Snakes cannot close their eyes, so you cannot tell if they are asleep. Mambas, cobras, bushmasters attack aggressively when cornered or guarding nests. |
| Use sticks | Turn logs and rocks with a stick before handling |
| Wear proper footgear | Especially at night |
| Check bedding, shelter, clothing | |
| Stay calm when encountering snakes | Snakes cannot hear; they will normally flee if given opportunity |
| Extreme care when killing for food/safety | Warm sleeping human bodies occasionally attract snakes |

**Venomous snakes of the world:**

| Region | Species |
|---|---|
| The Americas | Copperhead, bushmaster, coral snake, cottonmouth, fer-de-lance, rattlesnake |
| Europe | Common adder, Pallas' viper |
| Africa and Asia | Boomslang, cobra, Gaboon viper, green tree pit viper, habu pit viper, krait, Malayan pit viper, mamba, puff adder, rhinoceros viper, Russell's viper, sand viper, saw-scaled viper, Wagler's pit viper |
| Australia | Death adder, taipan, tiger snake, yellow-bellied sea snake |

**Snake-free areas:** polar regions, New Zealand, Cuba, Haiti, Jamaica, Puerto Rico, Ireland, Polynesia, Hawaii.

### DANGERS IN BAYS AND ESTUARIES

| Creature | Region | Hazard |
|---|---|---|
| Sea urchins | Shallow saltwaters | Pain and infection from stepping |
| Stingrays | Coasts of Americas, Africa, Australia | Venomous spike in tail; extremely painful wounds |

### SALTWATER DANGERS

| Fish | Region | Hazard |
|---|---|---|
| Sharks | All oceans | Bites, abrasions from rough skin |
| Rabbitfish (spinefoot) | Indian/Pacific coral reefs | Sharp, venomous spines in fins |
| Tang (surgeonfish) | Tropical seas | Scalpel-like spines in tail; infection, envenomation, blood loss |
| Toadfish | Gulf Coast, Central/South America coasts | Sharp, toxic spines along back |
| Scorpion fish (zebra fish) | Indian/Pacific reefs, Mediterranean/Aegean | Venomous spines; intensely painful sting |
| Stonefish | Indian/Pacific oceans | Venom from dorsal spines; near invisible on seafloor |
| Weever fish | Europe, Africa, Mediterranean coasts | Venomous spines on back and gills |
| Barracuda | Tropical seas | Ciguatera poison in flesh |
| Blowfish (puffer) | Tropical/temperate coasts worldwide | Blood, liver, gonads highly toxic; as little as 28mg can be fatal |
| Triggerfish | Tropical seas | Many have poisonous flesh |

**Toxic organs/flesh:**

| Animal | Toxic Part | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Polar bear | Liver | High vitamin A concentration; chance of death |
| Hawksbill turtle | Flesh | Down-turned bill, yellow polka dots on neck/flippers; unlikely to be captured |

### OTHER DANGEROUS SEA CREATURES

| Creature | Region | Hazard |
|---|---|---|
| Blue-ringed octopus | Great Barrier Reef, Australia | Deadly bite from parrotlike beak; small, grayish white with iridescent blue rings |
| Jellyfish (Portuguese man-of-war) | Tropical/subtropical seas | Poisonous tentacles up to 12m; stings extremely painful; most known jellyfish deaths attributed to this species |
| Cone shells | Indian/Pacific oceans (some lethal) | Venomous harpoonlike barb; avoid any shell shaped like an ice cream cone |
| Auger shells (terebra) | Temperate/tropical seas | Stinging barb; flesh may be poisonous |

> **CAUTION: All tropical octopus species should be treated with caution due to poisonous bites, though their flesh is edible. Avoid long tentacles of any jellyfish, even those washed up on the beach.**
