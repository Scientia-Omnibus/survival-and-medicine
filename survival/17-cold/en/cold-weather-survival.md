> One of the most difficult survival situations is a cold weather scenario. Every time you venture into the cold, you are pitting yourself against the elements. With a little knowledge of the environment, proper plans, and appropriate equipment, you can overcome the elements. As you remove one or more of these factors, survival becomes increasingly difficult. Remember, winter weather is highly variable. Prepare yourself to adapt to blizzard conditions even during sunny and clear weather. Cold is a far greater threat to survival than it appears. It decreases your ability to think and weakens your will to do anything except to get warm. Cold is an insidious enemy; as it numbs the mind and body, it subdues the will to survive.

## COLD REGIONS

| Environment | Temperature | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| Wet cold | Average 24hr: -10°C (14°F) or below | Freezing at night, thawing by day. Terrain is sloppy with slush and mud. |
| Dry cold | Average 24hr: remains below -10°C (14°F) | Much lower temperatures but no freeze/thaw cycle. Need more inner layers. Down to -60°C (-76°F). |

Windchill increases hazards. With 27.8 kph wind at -10°C (14°F), equivalent windchill is -23°C (-9°F). Movement (skiing, running) creates its own wind.

## BASIC PRINCIPLES — COLDER

| Letter | Principle | Detail |
|---|---|---|
| C | Keep clothing **C**lean | Dirt and grease crush air pockets, reducing insulation. |
| O | Avoid **O**verheating | Sweating dampens clothing, reducing insulation. Adjust layers, remove mittens, open hood. |
| L | Wear clothing **L**oose and in **L**ayers | Tight clothing restricts circulation. Layers trap dead air for insulation. |
| D | Keep clothing **D**ry | Inner layers wet from sweat; outer layers wet from snow. Use water-repellent outer layer. |
| E | **E**xamine uniform | Check for worn areas, tears, cleanliness. |
| R | **R**epair early | Fix tears before they grow. Improvise sewing kits from bones, plant fibers, 550 cord, thorns. |

Head cover is critical — 40–45% of body heat is lost from an unprotected head.

## COLD INJURIES

### Cold Injury Types

| Injury | Cause | Symptoms | Treatment |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hypothermia | Body temp drops faster than it can produce heat. General exposure or sudden wetting. | Shivering → uncontrollable shivering → sluggish thinking, irrational reasoning, false warmth → muscle rigidity, unconsciousness. Core <25°C (77°F): death likely. | Rewarm trunk in 37.7–43.3°C (100–110°F) water. Warm water enemas. Shared sleeping bag (both naked). Warm sweetened fluids if conscious. |
| Frostbite (light) | Frozen tissues, skin only. | Dull whitish pallor of skin. Loss of feeling for short time. | Warm with hands, mittens, armpits, or buddy's stomach. |
| Frostbite (deep) | Frozen tissues below skin. | Tissues solid and immovable. | Do not thaw unless definitive medical care available. Thawed and refrozen tissue causes more damage. |
| Trench foot / immersion foot | Hours/days of wet conditions just above freezing. | Pins and needles, tingling, numbness, pain. Skin: wet, soggy, white, shriveled → red → bluish/black. Feet cold, swollen, waxy. | Keep feet dry. Carry extra socks in waterproof packet. Dry wet socks against torso. Wash feet and put on dry socks daily. |

### Frostbite Prevention

| Body Part | Method |
|---|---|
| Face | Maintain circulation by making faces. Warm with hands. |
| Ears | Wiggle and move ears. Warm with hands. |
| Hands | Move hands inside gloves. Warm by placing close to body. |
| Feet | Move feet and wiggle toes inside boots. |

### Frostbite Do's and Don'ts

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| Periodically check for frostbite | Rub injury with snow |
| Rewarm light frostbite | Drink alcoholic beverages |
| Keep injured areas from refreezing | Smoke |
| | Try to thaw deep frostbite away from medical care |

## SHELTER TYPES

| Type | Construction | Best Use |
|---|---|---|
| Snow cave | Dig into drift ~3m deep. Arch roof. Sleeping platform higher than entrance. Ventilation shaft. | Most effective due to snow insulation. Takes time and energy. |
| Snow trench | Dig below snow/wind level. Use snow blocks or poncho for overhead cover. | Quick shelter in compacted snow. |
| Snow block and parachute | Snow block sides, parachute overhead. Clear snow regularly to prevent collapse. | When parachute material is available. |
| Snow house / igloo | Cut snow blocks, dome construction. Requires practice and proper equipment. | Traditional shelter for hunting/fishing. |
| Lean-to | Standard construction with snow piled around sides for insulation. | Quick in wooded areas. |
| Fallen tree | Dig snow out from under fallen tree. Use removed branches to line floor. | Minimal construction. |
| Tree-pit | Dig snow out from under large tree. Use branches to line floor. Ground sheet overhead. | 360-degree visibility. |

> **CAUTION:** In extreme cold, do not use metal shelters (e.g., aircraft fuselage). Metal conducts away heat. Never sleep directly on the ground. Always ventilate enclosed shelters. Never fall asleep with stove or lamp burning — carbon monoxide is colorless and odorless.

## FIRE

### Fuel Types

| Wood Type | Characteristics |
|---|---|
| Spruce (conifer) | Smoke-free in late fall/winter. Smoky in spring/summer. |
| Tamarack (conifer) | Loses needles in fall. Excellent for signaling — produces much smoke. |
| Birch (deciduous) | Burns hot and fast, as if oil-soaked. |
| Willow and alder | Burn hot and fast without much smoke. Found in marsh areas near water. |

### Fire Hazards

| Hazard | Precaution |
|---|---|
| Underground burning | Do not build fire too close to shelter. |
| Melting snow shelter | Excessive heat melts insulating layer. |
| Carbon monoxide | Ensure adequate ventilation. |
| Burns/scorching | Careful when drying clothes or getting warm. |
| Melting overhead snow | Can extinguish fire, wet equipment. |

## WATER

| Source | Method |
|---|---|
| Freshwater lakes, streams, ponds, rivers, springs | Purify before drinking. Running water usually fresh. |
| Tundra surface water (brownish) | Filter before purifying. |
| Freshwater ice and snow | Completely melt before putting in mouth. One cup ice yields more water than one cup snow. |
| Old sea ice | Rounded corners, bluish color. Nearly free of salt. |
| Body heat melting | Place snow in water bag between layers of clothing. Slow but works while moving. |

## TRAVEL

| Obstacle | Action |
|---|---|
| Blizzard | Avoid travel. |
| Thin ice | Distribute weight — lie flat and crawl. |
| Streams | Cross at lowest water level. Levels can vary 2–2.5m daily. |
| Whiteout conditions | Avoid travel. No contrasting colors to judge terrain. |
| Snow bridges | Cross at right angles. Probe ahead with pole. Crawl or use snowshoes/skis. |
| Deep snow | Use snowshoes or skis. Avoid snow-covered streams. |
| Avalanche areas | Travel in early morning. Avoid lee-side cornices. |
