> A tool is most useful when it is in good shape. This is especially true of your knife. You must always keep it sharp and ready to use. A knife is your most valuable tool in a survival situation. Imagine being in a survival situation without any weapons, tools, or equipment except your knife. It could happen! You might even be without a knife. You would probably feel helpless, but with the proper knowledge and skills, you can easily improvise needed items.
>
> In survival situations, you may have to fashion any number and type of field-expedient tools and equipment to survive. The need for an item must outweigh the work involved in making it. You should ask, "Is it necessary or just nice to have?" Remember that undue haste makes waste.

### STAFFS

A staff should be one of the first tools you obtain.

| Benefit | Description |
|---|---|
| Support | Helps in walking and ascending/descending steep slopes |
| Weapon | Effective against snakes and dogs |
| Eye protection | Moves through heavy brush and thorns in darkness |
| Size | Approximately your height (at least eyebrow height), no larger than you can effectively wield |

---

### CLUBS

Hold clubs — do not throw them. They extend your defense area and increase force of blow without injuring yourself.

| Type | Description | Construction |
|---|---|---|
| Simple club | Staff or branch, comfortable in palm, not too thin to break. Straight-grained hardwood is best. | Short enough to swing easily; long and strong enough to damage what you hit. |
| Weighted club | Simple club with weight on one end (natural knot or lashed stone). Stone with hourglass shape or grooved by pecking. | Find suitably shaped stone. Lash to hardwood handle of comfortable length using appropriate technique. |
| Sling club | Weight hangs 8-10cm from handle by strong flexible lashing. Extends reach and multiplies force. | Weight suspended from handle. |

### EDGED WEAPONS

**KNIFE MATERIALS**

| Material | Capabilities | Construction |
|---|---|---|
| Stone | Punctures and chops well; does not hold fine edge. Chert/flint have very fine edges. | Use chipping tool (wood/bone/metal) to rough out shape. Press flaking tool (bone/antler/soft iron) against edges to produce razor edge. Lash to hilt. |
| Bone | Punctures only; will not hold edge; may flake or break. | Shatter large bone with rock. Select suitable pointed splinter. Shape and sharpen on rough rock. Lash to hardwood handle if small. |
| Wood | Punctures only. Bamboo holds a suitable edge. | Select straight-grained hardwood ~30cm x 2.5cm. Fashion 15cm blade, shave to point. Fire-harden by drying over fire until lightly charred. Sharpen on coarse stone. For bamboo, keep outer layer (hardest part). |
| Metal | Best material — puncture, slice/chop, and cut. | Select suitable piece. Obtain point and edge by rubbing on rough stone. If soft enough, hammer out edge cold using flat hard surface as anvil. Make handle from wood/bone. |
| Glass | Good alternative; natural edge but less durable for heavy work. | Obtain suitable piece same as bone. |
| Plastic | Can be sharpened if thick/hard enough for puncturing. | |

**SPEAR BLADES**

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Make blade using same procedure as knife blade |
| 2 | Select straight sapling shaft 1.2-1.5m long |
| 3 | Attach blade to shaft using lashing (preferred: split handle, insert blade, wrap tightly) |
| 4 | Alternative: shave hardwood shaft to point, fire-harden. Bamboo: shave down end at 45-degree angle starting 8-10cm back; sharpen only inner portion. |

**ARROW POINTS**

Use same procedure as stone knife blade. Chert, flint, and shell-type stones are best. Bone can be flaked like stone. Broken glass also works.

### OTHER EXPEDIENT WEAPONS

| Weapon | Description | Target |
|---|---|---|
| Throwing stick (rabbit stick) | Blunt stick naturally curved at ~45 degrees. Heavy hardwood (oak). Shave two opposite sides flat like boomerang. | Small game (squirrels, chipmunks, rabbits) |
| Bow and arrow | See Food chapter for bow construction. Field-expedient bow has short lifespan. | Small to medium game |
| Bola | Weighted cords attached to center knot. Twirl above head and release. Wraps around target. | Running game, low-flying fowl in flocks |

**Throwing stick technique:**

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Align target by extending nonthrowing arm in line with mid- to lower-section |
| 2 | Raise throwing arm up and back until stick crosses back at ~45 degrees or is in line with nonthrowing hip |
| 3 | Bring throwing arm forward until just above and parallel to nonthrowing arm — this is the release point |
| 4 | Practice slowly and repeatedly for accuracy |

### CORDAGE AND LASHING

Many natural and man-made materials are suitable for cordage and lashing. Unravel a cotton web belt for string (fishing line, thread, lashing).

**NATURAL CORDAGE — Material selection test:**

| Test | Action |
|---|---|
| Strength | Pull on a length to test |
| Twist | Twist between fingers and roll fibers together |
| Knot | Tie overhand knot and gently tighten. If it does not break, material is usable. |

**LASHING MATERIALS**

| Material | Source | Use |
|---|---|---|
| Sinew | Tendons of large game (deer). Dry completely, smash to separate fibers, moisten and twist into strand. Braid for strength. | Best for small objects. No knots needed — moistened sinew is sticky and hardens when dry. |
| Plant fibers | Inner bark of linden, elm, hickory, white oak, mulberry, chestnut, red/white cedar. Shred and braid. | General cordage. Test for strength. Braid several strands together for more strength. |
| Rawhide | Skins of medium/large game. Remove fat/meat, dry completely. Cut dry hide into 6mm-wide strips starting from center in one continuous circular cut. Soak 2-4 hours until soft. Use wet, stretching as much as possible. | Larger lashing jobs. Strong and durable when dry. |

|---|---|---|

### CLOTHING AND INSULATION

**PARACHUTE ASSEMBLY**

Consider the entire parachute assembly as a resource. Before disassembling, consider all survival requirements: shelter, backpack, additional clothing/insulation.

**ANIMAL SKINS**

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Select hides of larger animals with heavier coats and large fat content |
| 2 | Avoid skins of infected/diseased animals |
| 3 | Clean thoroughly with water to remove pests (ticks, lice, fleas). If no water, shake out thoroughly. |
| 4 | Remove all fat and meat. Dry completely. |
| 5 | Use hindquarter joint areas for shoes, mittens, or socks. Wear with fur inside for insulation. |

**PLANT FIBER INSULATION**

| Plant | Source | Use |
|---|---|---|
| Cattail | Marshlands (lakes, ponds, river backwaters) | Fuzz on stalk tops forms dead air spaces; good down-like insulation between two layers of material |
| Milkweed | Various | Pollenlike seeds act as good insulation |
| Coconut husk fibers | Coconuts | Weave ropes; dried — excellent tinder and insulation |

### COOKING AND EATING UTENSILS

**BOWLS**

| Material | Method |
|---|---|
| Wood | Hollow out piece of wood. Hang over fire, add hot rocks to food/water. Remove cooled rocks and add more until cooked. |
| Bark or leaves | Same hot-rock method. Keep moist or keep fire low — will burn above waterline otherwise. |
| Bamboo | Cut out section between two sealed joints. |

> **CAUTION: Do not use rocks with air pockets (limestone, sandstone) — they may explode when heated. A sealed section of bamboo will explode if heated due to trapped air and water.**

**FORKS, KNIVES, AND SPOONS**

Carve from nonresinous woods (oak, birch, other hardwoods) to avoid resin aftertaste. Do not use trees that secrete syrup or resinlike liquid.

**POTS**

| Material | Method |
|---|---|
| Turtle shells | Thoroughly boil upper portion of shell. Use to heat food and water over flame. |
| Wood | Same hot-rock method as bowls. Bamboo is best wood for cooking containers. |

**WATER BOTTLES**

Use stomachs of larger animals. Thoroughly flush with water, tie off bottom. Leave top open with some means of fastening closed.
