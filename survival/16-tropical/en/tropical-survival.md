> Most people think of the tropics as a huge and forbidding tropical rain forest through which every step taken must be hacked out, and where every inch of the way is crawling with danger. Actually, over half of the land in the tropics is cultivated in some way. A knowledge of field skills, the ability to improvise, and the application of the principles of survival will increase the prospects of survival. Do not be afraid of being alone in the jungle; fear will lead to panic. Panic will lead to exhaustion and decrease your chance of survival. Everything in the jungle thrives, including disease germs and parasites that breed at an alarming rate. Nature will provide water, food, and plenty of materials to build shelters. Indigenous peoples have lived for millennia by hunting and gathering. However, it will take an outsider some time to get used to the conditions and the nonstop activity of tropical survival.

## TROPICAL WEATHER

| Factor | Description |
|---|---|
| Temperature | High temps, oppressive humidity. Variation seldom less than 10°C (50°F), often more than 35°C (95°F) at low altitudes. Ice often forms at night above 1,500m. |
| Rainfall | Heavy, often with thunder and lightning. Sudden rain turns trickles into raging torrents. Violent storms occur toward end of summer. |
| Hurricanes/cyclones/typhoons | Develop over sea, rush inland causing tidal waves and devastation. Choose campsites above potential flooding. |
| Prevailing winds | Vary between winter and summer. Dry season has rain once daily; monsoon has continuous rain. |
| Day/night | Equal length. Darkness falls quickly; daybreak is sudden. |

## JUNGLE TYPES

| Jungle Type | Characteristics | Travel / Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Tropical rain forest | Climate varies little. Up to 3.5m (12ft) rain yearly. Five vegetation layers. Dense canopy blocks light. | Little undergrowth on floor. Visibility ~50m. Easy to lose direction. Hard for aircraft to see you. |
| Secondary jungle | Similar to rain forest. Prolific growth where sunlight reaches floor. Found along riverbanks, fringes, cleared areas. | Tangled vegetation reclaims cleared areas quickly. May find cultivated food plants. |
| Semievergreen seasonal and monsoon forests | Two stories of tree strata (18–24m upper, 7–13m lower). Leaves fall during seasonal drought. | Same edible plants as rain forest except sago, nipa, coconut palms. |
| Scrub and thorn forests | Definite dry season. Trees leafless in dry season. Thorns predominate. Fires frequent. | Hard to obtain food plants in dry season. Abundant in rainy season. |
| Savannas | Broad grassy meadow with widely spaced trees. Often red soil. Trees appear stunted and gnarled. | Found in tropical South America and Africa. |
| Saltwater swamps | Coastal areas subject to tidal flooding. Mangrove trees up to 12m with tangled roots. | Movement extremely difficult. Avoid if possible. Use raft if water channels exist. |
| Freshwater swamps | Low-lying inland areas. Thorny undergrowth, reeds, grasses, short palms. Islands may provide dry spots. | Wildlife abundant. Travel difficult. |

## TRAVEL THROUGH JUNGLE AREAS

| Principle | Technique |
|---|---|
| Develop "jungle eye" | Focus on jungle further out, not immediate front. Find natural breaks in foliage. Look through the jungle, not at it. |
| Use game trails | Trail wind and cross but frequently lead to water or clearings. |
| Use machete wisely | Cut upward when cutting vines to reduce noise. Do not cut unnecessarily. |
| Use a stick | Part vegetation to dislodge biting ants, spiders, or snakes. |
| Follow utility lines | Right-of-way is often clear enough for easy travel. |
| Wear long sleeves | Avoid cuts and scratches. |

### Travel Tips

| Tip | Detail |
|---|---|
| Pinpoint location | Determine general line of travel to safety. Use field-expedient direction finding if no compass. |
| Take stock | Inventory water supplies and equipment. |
| Move in one direction | Not necessarily straight line. Avoid obstacles. Use natural cover in enemy territory. |
| Move smoothly | Turn shoulders, shift hips, bend body, shorten/lengthen stride to slide through undergrowth. |

## IMMEDIATE CONSIDERATIONS

| Priority | Action |
|---|---|
| Take from crash site | Machete, compass, first aid kit, parachute or material for mosquito netting and shelter. |
| Seek shelter | Protect from tropical rain, sun, and insects. |
| Protect from mosquitoes | Malaria-carrying mosquitoes and other insects are immediate dangers. |
| Mark route | Blaze or mark your route carefully. Use compass. Know your direction. |
| Treat wounds promptly | Even the smallest scratch can become dangerously infected. |

## WATER PROCUREMENT

### Signs of Water From Animals

| Animal | Indicator |
|---|---|
| Grazing animals (deer) | Never far from water. Drink at dawn and dusk. Converging trails often lead to water. |
| Carnivores | Not reliable indicators. Get moisture from prey. |
| Grain-eating birds (finches, pigeons) | Never far from water. Drink at dawn and dusk. Fly straight and low when heading to water. |
| Water birds | Not reliable. Fly long distances without stopping. |
| Bees | Seldom range more than 6km from hive. Usually have water source within that range. |
| Ants | Column marching up a tree indicates trapped water in the tree. |
| Flies (European mason fly) | Stay within 100m of water. Iridescent green body. |
| Human tracks | Usually lead to a well, bore hole, or soak. Replace cover after use. |

### Water From Plants

| Source | Method |
|---|---|
| Vines | Rough bark, ~5cm thick. Nonpoisonous = clear fluid. Poisonous = sticky, milky sap. Let liquid drip into mouth or container. |
| Roots (water tree, desert oak, bloodwood) | Pry out roots, cut into 30cm lengths. Remove bark and suck moisture, or shave to pulp and squeeze. |
| Palm trees (buri, coconut, nipa) | Bend flowering stalk downward, cut off tip. Cut thin slice every 12 hours to renew flow. Collect up to 1 liter per day. |
| Condensation (solar still) | Tie clear plastic bag around green leafy branch. Cut vegetation in a bag also produces condensation. |
| Muddy streams or lakes | Dig hole in sandy soil ~1m from bank. Water seeps in. Must purify. |

## FOOD

Food is usually abundant. Supplement diet with edible plants. Best foraging areas are banks of streams and rivers where sun penetrates.

| Guideline | Detail |
|---|---|
| Conserve energy | Do not climb or fell trees for food if weak. Choose easily obtained sources near ground. |
| Pick only what you need | Food spoils rapidly in tropical conditions. Leave on growing plant until needed. |
| Start with known plants | Begin with palms, bamboos, and common fruits unless you can positively identify others. |
| Poisonous plants | Proportion is no greater than other regions, but density of growth makes them appear more numerous. |
