> A shelter can protect you from the sun, insects, wind, rain, snow, hot or cold temperatures, and enemy observation. It can give you a feeling of well-being and help you maintain your will to survive.
>
> In some areas, your need for shelter may take precedence over your need for food and possibly even your need for water. For example, prolonged exposure to cold can cause excessive fatigue and weakness (exhaustion). An exhausted person may develop a "passive" outlook, thereby losing the will to survive.
>
> Seek natural shelters or alter them to meet your needs, therefore, saving energy. A common error in making a shelter is to make it too large. A shelter must be large enough to protect you and small enough to contain your body heat, especially in cold climates.

### PRIMARY SHELTER

Your primary shelter in a survival situation will be what you wear. This is true regardless of whether you are in a hot, cold, tropical, desert, or arctic situation. For your clothes to protect you, they must be in good condition and worn properly.

### SHELTER SITE SELECTION

When you realize shelter is a high priority, start looking as soon as possible. Two requisites for shelter are that it must:

- Contain material to make the type of shelter you need.
- Be large enough and level enough for you to lie down comfortably.

The site should also:

- Provide concealment.
- Have escape routes.
- Be suitable for signaling, if necessary.
- Provide protection against wild animals and rocks and dead trees that might fall.
- Be free from insects, reptiles, and poisonous plants.

Avoid:

- Flash flood areas in foothills.
- Avalanche or rockslide areas in mountainous terrain.
- Sites near bodies of water that are below the high-water mark.

During cold winter months, select a site that protects from cold and wind but has a source of fuel and water. During summer, seek a water source and an insect-free site.

Remember the word **BLISS**:

- **B**lend in with the surroundings.
- **L**ow silhouette.
- **I**rregular shape.
- **S**mall.
- **S**ecluded location.

### TYPES OF SHELTERS

When selecting a shelter type, consider:

- How much time and effort is needed to build it?
- Will it adequately protect from the elements (sun, wind, rain, snow)?
- Do you have the tools to build it? If not, can you improvise?
- Do you have the type and amount of materials needed?

| Shelter Type | Materials | Best For | Limitations |
|---|---|---|---|
| Poncho Lean-To | Poncho, 2-3m rope, 3 stakes, 2 trees/poles | Quick shelter, rain/wind protection | Open on one side |
| Poncho Tent | Poncho, 2 ropes, 6 stakes, 2 trees | Low silhouette, two-sided protection | Less space, limited observation |
| 3-Pole Parachute Tepee | Parachute, 3 poles 3.5-4.5m | Multiple people, fire inside, signaling | Large, conspicuous |
| 1-Pole Parachute Tepee | 14-gore canopy, stakes, center pole | Stable shelter | More materials and assembly |
| No-Pole Parachute Tepee | Parachute, stakes, suitable tree | Uses existing tree | Requires suitable tree |
| One-Man Shelter | Parachute, 3 poles, tree | Small, easily warmed | Caves in under snow |
| Field-Expedient Lean-To | Natural materials, knife optional | Wooded areas, no tools needed | Time-consuming |
| Swamp Bed | 4 trees/poles, cross poles, leaves | Marsh/swamp, above water | Requires sturdy supports |
| Debris Hut | Ridgepole, sticks, debris 1m+ thick | Maximum warmth | Large debris volume needed |
| Tree-Pit Snow Shelter | Snow, evergreen tree, digging tool | Cold, snow-covered areas | Requires digging tool |
| Beach Shade Shelter | Driftwood, natural materials | Beach sun/wind/rain protection | Above high-water mark |
| Desert Shelter | Poncho/canvas, sand/rocks | Arid environments, sun protection | Heat management |

**PONCHO LEAN-TO**

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Tie off poncho hood: pull drawstring tight, roll hood longways, fold into thirds, tie off |
| 2 | Cut rope in half. Tie each half to the corner grommet on one long side of the poncho |
| 3 | Attach a drip stick (~10cm) to each rope ~2.5cm from the grommet to keep rain from running down ropes |
| 4 | Tie ropes about waist high on trees using a round turn and two half hitches with quick-release |
| 5 | Spread poncho and anchor to ground with sharpened sticks through grommets |

For multi-night use or rain, add a center support line from poncho hood to an overhanging branch, or place an upright stick under center. Add brush or equipment at sides for wind protection. Place insulating material (leaves, pine needles) inside.

> When at rest, you lose as much as 80 percent of your body heat to the ground.

For lower silhouette: secure support lines at knee height and angle the poncho to the ground.

**PONCHO TENT**

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Tie off poncho hood same as lean-to |
| 2 | Tie a 1.5-2.5m rope to the center grommet on each side |
| 3 | Tie rope ends at knee height to two trees 2-3m apart, stretch poncho tight |
| 4 | Draw one side tight and secure with sharpened sticks through grommets |
| 5 | Repeat for the other side |

For center support, use an A-frame of two 90-120cm sticks (one forked) set outside over center. Tie hood drawstring to A-frame.

**THREE-POLE PARACHUTE TEPEE**

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Lay three poles (3.5-4.5m) on ground, lash together at one end |
| 2 | Stand framework up, spread poles into tripod |
| 3 | Add 5-6 additional poles for support (do not lash) |
| 4 | Determine wind direction; locate entrance 90+ degrees from mean wind |
| 5 | Lay parachute on backside of tripod, locate bridle loop at apex of canopy |
| 6 | Place bridle loop over top of a freestanding pole; set pole against tripod so apex matches lashing height |
| 7 | Wrap canopy around one side of tripod (double thickness) |
| 8 | Construct entrance by wrapping folded edges around two freestanding poles |
| 9 | Place extra canopy underneath poles for floor |
| 10 | Leave 30-50cm opening at top for ventilation if using fire inside |

One-pole version: scribe ~4m circle, stake material to ground using remaining suspension lines, attach to center pole, sew end gores leaving 1-1.2m for door.

No-pole version: tie line to parachute top, throw over tree limb, stake starting opposite door, then tighten and secure to tree trunk.

**ONE-MAN SHELTER**

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Secure 4.5m pole to tree at waist height |
| 2 | Lay two 3m poles on ground on either side, same direction as 4.5m pole |
| 3 | Lay folded canopy over 4.5m pole so equal material hangs both sides |
| 4 | Tuck excess under 3m poles, spread inside for floor |
| 5 | Stake or put a spreader between the 3m poles at entrance |
| 6 | Use excess material to cover entrance |

Parachute cloth makes this wind-resistant. A candle can keep it warm. Unsatisfactory in snowfall.

**PARACHUTE HAMMOCK** — Use 6-8 gores of parachute canopy and two trees ~4.5m apart.

**FIELD-EXPEDIENT LEAN-TO**

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Tie a 2m pole between two trees at waist-to-chest height (horizontal support) |
| 2 | Place one end of 3m beam poles on one side of horizontal support, back into wind |
| 3 | Crisscross saplings or vines on the beams |
| 4 | Cover framework with brush, leaves, pine needles, or grass, starting at bottom and working up (shingling) |
| 5 | Place straw, leaves, or pine needles inside for bedding |

For cold weather: build a fire reflector wall. Drive four 1.5m stakes, stack green logs between them in two rows, fill inner space with dirt, bind tops. For a drying rack: span poles between lean-to support and reflector wall top.

**SWAMP BED**

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Find four trees in a rectangle, or drive four poles firmly into ground |
| 2 | Cut two strong poles spanning the rectangle's width |
| 3 | Secure them to trees/poles high enough above ground/water for tides |
| 4 | Lay additional poles across the length, secure them |
| 5 | Cover top with broad leaves or grass for soft sleeping surface |
| 6 | Build fire pad by laying clay, silt, or mud on one corner; allow to dry |

Alternative: lay sticks and branches lengthwise inside the rectangle until the sleeping surface is above water level.

**NATURAL SHELTERS**

Consider caves, rocky crevices, clumps of bushes, depressions, large rocks on leeward hillsides, large trees with low limbs, fallen trees with thick branches. When selecting:

| Criteria | Action |
|---|---|
| Low ground | Avoid — collects cold air and insects |
| Pests | Check for poisonous snakes, ticks, mites, scorpions, stinging ants |
| Falling hazards | Check for loose rocks, dead limbs, coconuts |

**DEBRIS HUT**

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Build tripod with two short stakes and a long ridgepole, or place one end of ridgepole on a sturdy base |
| 2 | Secure ridgepole at about waist height |
| 3 | Prop large sticks along both sides of ridgepole to create wedge-shaped ribbing (wide enough for body, steep enough to shed moisture) |
| 4 | Place finer sticks and brush crosswise on ribbing for latticework |
| 5 | Add light, dry debris over ribbing at least 1m thick |
| 6 | Place 30cm layer of insulating material inside |
| 7 | Pile insulating material at entrance to close it off (or build a door) |
| 8 | Add shingling or branches on top to prevent debris blowing away |

**TREE-PIT SNOW SHELTER**

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Find an evergreen tree with bushy branches for overhead cover |
| 2 | Dig out snow around trunk to desired depth/diameter or to ground |
| 3 | Pack snow around top and inside of hole for support |
| 4 | Place evergreen boughs over top for cover; place boughs in bottom for insulation |

**BEACH SHADE SHELTER**

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Collect driftwood or natural material for supports and digging |
| 2 | Select site above high-water mark |
| 3 | Scrape/dig a trench running north-south, long and wide enough to lie in |
| 4 | Mound soil on three sides of trench |
| 5 | Lay support beams across trench on top of mound for roof framework |
| 6 | Enlarge entrance by digging out more sand in front |
| 7 | Use grass or leaves for bedding inside |

**DESERT SHELTERS**

Using rock outcroppings: anchor one end of material on outcrop edge with rocks, extend other end for shade.

In sandy areas: build sand mound or use dune side for one wall, anchor material on top, extend for shade.

> If enough material, fold in half to create 30-45cm airspace between layers to reduce temperature.

Belowground shelter: dig trench 45-60cm deep in low spot, pile sand on three sides, cover with material, secure with sand/rocks. Can reduce midday heat 16-22°C. Extra layer 30-45cm above reduces temperature further.

Open desert shelter: all sides open to air currents. Minimum two layers of parachute material. White best for heat reflection; inner layer darker.
