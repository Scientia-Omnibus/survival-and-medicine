> Sea survival is perhaps the most difficult survival situation. Short- or long-term survival depends upon rations, equipment available, and your ingenuity. You must be resourceful to survive. Water covers about 75 percent of the earth's surface, with about 70 percent being oceans and seas. You can assume that you will sometime cross vast expanses of water. There is always the chance that the plane or ship you are on will become crippled by such hazards as storms, collision, fire, or war.

## THE OPEN SEA

### Precautionary Measures

Survival at sea depends upon knowledge of and ability to use survival equipment, special skills to cope with hazards, and the will to live. When boarding a ship or aircraft, find out what survival equipment is on board, where it is stowed, and what it contains.

### Actions When Down at Sea

| Action | Detail |
|---|---|
| Get clear | Move upwind of aircraft. Stay in vicinity until aircraft sinks. |
| Avoid fuel | Get clear of fuel-covered water in case of ignition. |
| Find others | Try to locate other survivors. |
| Reach a raft | If no raft, find floating debris. Relax — natural buoyancy keeps head above water. |

### Swimming Strokes

| Stroke | Best Use |
|---|---|
| Dog paddle | Excellent when clothed or wearing life jacket. Low energy. |
| Breaststroke | Swimming underwater, through oil/debris, or in rough seas. Best for long-range. |
| Sidestroke | Good relief stroke. Uses one arm. |
| Backstroke | Excellent relief stroke. Use if underwater explosion is likely. |

### Raft Procedures — Five As

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| **A**ir | Check all chambers inflated. Close all inflation valves. Clamp equalization tubes. |
| **A**ssistance | Help others into raft. Remove puncture-producing items. |
| **A**nchor | Deploy sea anchor properly. |
| **A**ccessory bag | Locate and retrieve accessory bag. |
| **A**ssessment | Assess situation. Keep positive mental attitude. |

## COLD WEATHER CONSIDERATIONS

| Measure | Detail |
|---|---|
| Antiexposure suit | Put on if available. Otherwise, add extra clothing, keep loose. |
| Protect raft | Avoid snagging with shoes or sharp objects. |
| Windbreak | Rig spray shield and canopy. |
| Keep dry | Cover raft floor with canvas or cloth for insulation. |
| Huddle | Share body heat. Move enough to keep blood circulating. |
| Extra rations | Give to those suffering from cold exposure. |

### Cold Water Survival Time

| Water Temp | Expected Survival Time |
|---|---|
| 21.0–15.5°C (70–60°F) | 12 hours |
| 15.5–10.0°C (60–50°F) | 6 hours |
| 10.0–4.5°C (50–40°F) | 1 hour |
| 4.5°C (40°F) and below | Less than 1 hour |

Antiexposure suit may increase times up to 24 hours.

## WATER PROCUREMENT

### Water Sources

| Method | Detail |
|---|---|
| Short-water rations | Shade body from overhead sun and reflection. Ventilate. Dampen clothes during hottest part. Rest. |
| Rain catching | Keep tarpaulin handy. Wash salt off in seawater first. At night, turn up edges to collect dew. |
| Manual reverse osmosis desalinator (MROD) | Pumps 6–35 gallons potable water in 24 hours. 2-second cycle. Purge antimicrobial agent for 2 minutes first. |
| Solar still | Set up immediately. Use as many as possible. Only works on flat, calm seas. Secure to raft. |
| Desalting kits | Use for immediate needs or during overcast periods when solar stills cannot be used. |
| Water from fish | Aqueous fluid along spine and in eyes of large fish. Do not drink other body fluids. |
| Sea ice (old) | Bluish, rounded corners, splinters easily. Nearly salt-free. New ice is gray, milky, hard, salty. |

### Water Rules — Do NOT

| Action | Reason |
|---|---|
| Drink seawater | Accelerates dehydration. |
| Drink urine | Toxic waste, accelerates dehydration. |
| Drink alcohol | Increases dehydration. |
| Smoke | Increases water needs. |
| Eat unless water available | Digestion consumes water. |

## FOOD PROCUREMENT

### Fish Types

| Type | Edibility | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Most open-sea fish | Safe | When out of sight of land, generally safe. |
| Red snapper, barracuda (reef/atoll) | Potentially poisonous | Normally edible but may be toxic near reefs and atolls. |
| Shark | Good source | Bleed immediately, soak in several changes of water. Spoils rapidly. Do not eat Greenland shark or livers. |
| Eels | Edible | Handle carefully. Do not confuse with sea snakes (scaly body, paddle-shaped tail). |
| Sea turtles | Edible | All parts can be used. |

### Fishing Aids

| Material | Improvisation |
|---|---|
| Fishing line | Unravel tarpaulin or canvas threads, tie in groups of 3+. Shoelaces or parachute suspension line. |
| Fish hooks | Improvise from available materials. |
| Fish lures | Attach double hook to any shiny piece of metal. |
| Grapple | Use to hook seaweed. Shake out crabs, shrimp, or small fish. |
| Bait | Small fish, guts from birds and fish. Keep bait moving to appear alive. |

### Fishing Hints

| Hint | Detail |
|---|---|
| Watch for teeth and spines | Handle all fish with care. |
| Cut large fish loose | Risk of capsizing raft. Target small fish. |
| Protect raft | Avoid puncturing with hooks or sharp objects. |
| Avoid fishing near sharks | Dangerous. |
| Fish at night | Use light to attract fish. |
| Shade attracts fish | Fish may be under your raft during day. |
| Improvise spear | Tie knife to oar blade. Retrieve fish quickly. |

### Birds

All sea birds are edible. Catch any that land on or near the raft. Use a bird noose: bait the center, pull tight when feet are in position. Use all parts — feathers for insulation, entrails for bait.

## MEDICAL PROBLEMS

| Problem | Symptoms | Treatment |
|---|---|---|
| Seasickness | Nausea, vomiting, fluid loss, exhaustion | Wash patient and raft. No food until nausea passes. Lie down and rest. Seasickness pills before symptoms appear. |
| Saltwater sores | Broken skin from prolonged saltwater exposure, clothing bind points | Flush with freshwater, dry. Apply antiseptic. Do not open or drain. |
| Immersion rot / frostbite / hypothermia | Same as cold weather environments | Same treatment as cold weather. |
| Blindness / headache | Flame, smoke, contaminants in eyes; glare inflammation | Flush with saltwater then freshwater. Bandage 18–24 hours. Wear sunglasses. |
| Constipation | Common on raft | Do not take laxative (causes dehydration). Exercise, drink adequate water. |
| Sunburn | Serious problem at sea | Stay in shade. Cover head and skin. Use cream or lip salve. |

## SHARKS

### Dangerous Species

| Species | Notes |
|---|---|
| Great white, hammerhead, mako, tiger | Most dangerous. |
| Gray, blue, lemon, sand, nurse, bull, oceanic white-tip | Known to attack. |
| Any shark over 1m (3ft) | Consider dangerous. |

### Protection in Water

| Measure | Detail |
|---|---|
| Stay with group | 360-degree watch. Group can frighten or fight off sharks. |
| Wear all clothing | Sharks attack unclothed men first. Clothing also protects from abrasion. |
| Avoid urinating | If necessary, small amounts, let dissipate. |
| If attacked | Kick and strike gills or eyes. Hitting nose may injure hand on teeth. |

### Protection in Raft

| Measure | Detail |
|---|---|
| Do not fish | Release any hooked fish. Do not clean fish in water. |
| No garbage overboard | Attracts sharks. |
| Keep limbs in raft | Do not let arms, legs, or equipment hang in water. |
| Stay quiet | Avoid movement. |
| Bury dead promptly | At night if many sharks present. |

## DETECTING LAND

| Sign | Detail |
|---|---|
| Fixed cumulus cloud | Hovers over or slightly downwind from land. |
| Greenish sky tint | Reflection from shallow lagoons or coral reefs (tropics). |
| Light cloud reflections | Indicate ice fields or snow-covered land (arctic). |
| Water color | Deep = dark green/blue. Light = shallow, land may be near. |
| Odors and sounds | Musty odor of mangrove swamps. Roar of surf. Bird cries from one direction. |
| Bird flight patterns | Dawn = toward land. Dusk = toward land. Day = feeding, no significance. |
| Wave refraction | Pattern changes as waves approach land. Travel with waves parallel to turbulent area. |

## RAFTING / BEACHING TECHNIQUES

| Condition | Technique |
|---|---|
| Strong surf | Take down mast. Keep clothes and shoes on. Trail sea anchor. Use oars. |
| Medium surf, no wind | Keep raft from passing over waves too rapidly. |
| Approaching beach | Ride in on crest of large wave. Paddle hard. Do not jump until raft grounds. |
| Night landing | Avoid if possible. Signal and wait if people are on shore. |
| Sea ice | Land only on large, stable floes. Avoid icebergs. Keep raft inflated. |
| Rocky shore | Look for waves rushing up onto rocks, not exploding spray. Sit with feet forward to absorb shock. |
| Coral reef | Cross like rocky shore. Keep feet together, knees slightly bent. |

## PICKUP OR RESCUE

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| Clear lines | Remove fishing lines, desalting kit lines. |
| Secure items | Stow all loose gear. Take down canopies and sails. |
| Prepare for helicopter | Secure loose equipment. Deploy sea anchor. Partially deflate raft. Grasp handhold, roll out. Mount recovery device. Signal with thumb up. Do not reach for crew. |
| Follow instructions | Rescue personnel will guide you. |
